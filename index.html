Literature Review

Usability Study

The International Organization for Standardization states that usability is an outcome of use defined as when a system, product or service can be used by users to achieve effectiveness, efficiency and satisfaction of context usage(ISO, 2018).

Three experiments completed about the usability evaluation study reports that 80% of all usability problems can be detected by the first four to five participants. Having more will not improve the information because the most impactful problems are usually detected within the first four to five subjects(Virzi, 1992). This means that for our usability study, we need to have at least four participants for an effective test.

Thematic Analysis

Braun and Clarke (2006) defines thematic analysis as a method for analysing qualitative data that entails scouring over a data set to identify recurring patterns and examining and documenting them.

Thematic analysis applies to a wide range of study questions, designs, and sample sizes, as well as used in a wide range of theoretical and epistemological frameworks. This is one of its distinguishing characteristics.

When attempting to comprehend a group of experiences, thoughts, or behaviours spread across a data set, thematic analysis is an appropriate and effective technique to employ. According to Sandelowski and Barroso (2003), the degree of data transformation during analysis determines where on a continuum qualitative analysis methods fall.

Thematic Analysis allows you to code and categorise data into themes. Thematic Analysis allows for the display and classification of processed data based on similarities and differences. (1994, Miles and Huberman). According to Braun and Clarke (2006), a theme is a "patterned response or meaning" derived from the data that answers the research question. The frequency of a theme's appearance in the data does not always correspond to how important or central it is. Since Braun and Clarke's (2006) method of thematic analysis has been adopted the most frequently in the qualitative literature, we will concentrate on it (Clarke and Braun 2017).

The inductive approach involves deriving meaning and creating themes from data without any preconceptions. The majority of the data that is collected will start with a precise content and then move to broader generalisations and theories. This tends to ensure the themes are effectively linked to the data (Patton, 1990).

Conclusion

Following the completion of the Oodle usability study, a number of issues with varying degrees of urgency have been identified. This includes, but is not limited to, clarity, lack of trust, Content and navigation through the site. These themes display the issue-related patterns contained within the site. Overall, it is clear that Oodle, as discussed in the previous paragraph, necessitates a significant transformation in its usability, as it is currently more of an annoyance to utilize than an actually useful application. As a result, in terms of the research question, Oodle is not a useful application with current usability, and it is suggested that various changes be implemented, as discussed above. However, as mentioned in the previous section, another usability study should be carried out after such changes are implemented in order to determine the newer technologies in terms of usability and then iterate this process as needed. Subsequently, this would likely lead to Oodle improving and possibly evolving into a useful web application.
